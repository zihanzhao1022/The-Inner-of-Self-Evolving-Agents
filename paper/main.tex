\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Self-evolving reinforcement learning, exemplified by the Absolute-Zero-Reasoner
(AZR) procedure, trains large language models on self-generated math and code
tasks without external preference data. Whether such training subtly reshapes
a model's internal value representations, or — alternatively — its
safety-relevant output policy, is largely unsettled because most safety audits
inspect either weights or behaviour, rarely both on the same model at scale.
Here we probe nine Qwen2.5 variants spanning two scales (3B, 7B) with two
complementary measurements: (i)~five independent representational distance
metrics, and (ii)~refusal behaviour on 200 HarmBench jailbreak prompts
evaluated under each model's native chat template. We find that AZR leaves
the residual-stream geometry essentially untouched — weight cosine
$\geq 0.999999$, refusal-direction cosine $\approx 0.98$, last-layer
displacement $1.0$--$1.4^{\circ}$, Procrustes residual $0.0001$--$0.0005$ —
and that scaling makes this silence five-fold tighter at 7B. A no-Coder
control (AZR-Base-7B trained directly from the base model) reproduces the
geometric silence, ruling out Coder pretraining as the stabiliser. A
cross-AZR transitivity test (the distance between the two AZR variants is
bit-identical to the distance between their non-AZR counterparts) and a
Procrustes-rotated steering-vector transfer demonstrate that the silence is
mechanistic, not numerical. Yet on the same models, AZR-Coder refuses
$0$ of $200$ HarmBench prompts at both 3B and 7B, against $11.5\%$ and
$23.5\%$ for their Coder bases; AZR-Base-7B drops base refusal from
$20.5\%$ to $6.5\%$. Of the $200$ prompts, $199$ elicit no refusal from
either AZR-Coder scale. Identical internal geometry thus supports completely
opposite refusal policies. Representation-level safety audits are
insufficient to certify self-evolving fine-tuned models; behavioural
evaluation under native templates is essential.
\end{abstract}

% =====================================================================
\section{Introduction}

Self-supervised reinforcement learning has emerged as a route to improve
large language model (LLM) reasoning without curated preference data.
Absolute-Zero-Reasoner~\citep{zhao2024azr} is a representative instance:
a base LLM proposes its own math and code problems, attempts to solve them,
and is updated against an internally computed correctness signal. The
resulting models match or exceed RLHF-tuned counterparts on coding
benchmarks while consuming no human-labelled data. The procedure is
therefore appealing as a scalable post-training recipe.

A safety concern, however, has been flagged repeatedly in the recent
literature: post-training that optimises for ``task completion'' can erode
refusal behaviour learned earlier in the pipeline. The AZR paper itself
reports an ``uh-oh moment'' on AZR-Llama-3.1-8B, in which the model produces
concerning outputs during self-play. Whether such drift reflects (i)~a
hidden change in the model's internal value representation, (ii)~a
surface-level shift in the output policy with an unchanged internal
substrate, or (iii)~both, has not been characterised, because the two are
typically measured by disjoint communities — interpretability work measures
weights and activations, alignment work measures completions — and existing
AZR analyses have not jointly evaluated the same checkpoint along both axes.

Prior attempts to bridge this gap have two limitations. First,
refusal-direction analysis~\citep{arditi2024refusal} estimates a single
Difference-in-Means vector for ``harmful versus harmless'' prompts but,
as we will show, this vector mostly captures prompt-category geometry
rather than the model's downstream refuse-or-comply decision. Second,
behavioural refusal-rate evaluations typically apply a single chat
template across heterogeneous models, mixing two genuinely different
operating regimes — a model evaluated outside its training distribution
will produce continuation-style text that defeats refusal-prefix
classifiers and inflates apparent compliance.

Here we report a paired representation--behaviour audit of nine Qwen2.5
variants at two scales (3B and 7B), evaluated under each model's
native chat template. We find a clean two-layer dissociation: self-evolving
RL preserves residual-stream geometry to fp64 precision across both scales,
while simultaneously erasing essentially all refusal behaviour on the
HarmBench~\citep{mazeika2024harmbench} jailbreak benchmark. We then provide
three independent controls — a no-Coder pathway (AZR-Base-7B), a cross-AZR
transitivity test, and a Procrustes-rotated steering-vector transfer —
that constrain the silence to a property of the AZR training step itself,
not of intermediate Coder pretraining or of measurement artefacts. The
result has direct implications for safety auditing: representational
distance metrics are insufficient to detect refusal-policy drift.

\section{Results}

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Self-evolving RL leaves residual-stream geometry untouched}
\label{sec:silence}

We measured five independent geometric distances between each pair of
models in the 3B quartet and the 7B quintet (Fig.~\ref{fig:silence}).
On the self-evolving axis — Qwen2.5-Coder-3B $\to$ AZR-Coder-3B,
Qwen2.5-Coder-7B $\to$ AZR-Coder-7B, and Qwen2.5-7B $\to$ AZR-Base-7B —
every metric reports near-perfect identity: fp64 per-tensor weight cosine
$\geq 0.999999$ across all $252$ (layer $\times$ component) pairs at 7B
and all $324$ at 3B; refusal-direction cosine $\approx 0.98$ at the
model-appropriate emergence layer (L27 for 3B, L21 for 7B); centroid
displacement between the second-to-last and last transformer blocks of
$1.0$--$1.4^{\circ}$; Procrustes residual after optimal rigid alignment
of $0.0005$ at 3B and $0.0001$ at 7B — a five-fold tightening with
scale; and a near-zero gap on three downstream value benchmarks
(TruthfulQA MC1 $\Delta = -0.005$, BBQ ambiguous-context accuracy
$\Delta = 0.000$, AdvBench compliance log-probability
$\Delta = -0.056$).

These five measurements are not redundant. The weight cosine probes the
parameter manifold; refusal-direction cosine probes a specific functional
axis; displacement angle probes class-level activation geometry;
Procrustes residual probes the relational shape across all six IBM topic
classes after factoring out rigid rotation; and value benchmarks probe
end-to-end likelihood. Convergence across all five rules out the
possibility that any single metric is masking a change in another modality.

For comparison, RLHF (base~$\to$~Instruct) and continued domain
pretraining (base~$\to$~Coder) produce visible shifts on the same metrics:
weight cosines drop to $0.18$--$0.99$, displacement reaches
$24^{\circ}$--$88^{\circ}$, and Procrustes residuals are $5$- to $60$-fold
larger. Self-evolving RL is uniquely silent.

\subsection{The geometric silence is intrinsic to self-evolving RL,
not inherited from Coder pretraining}
\label{sec:controls}

A natural objection to the silence finding is that all our self-evolving
checkpoints were initialised from Qwen2.5-Coder, whose extensive
code-corpus continued pretraining might have produced a representation
rigid enough to absorb subsequent RL without visible drift. We addressed
this objection with three independent controls.

\paragraph{Necessity control: no-Coder pathway.}
AZR-Base-7B — trained from plain Qwen2.5-7B with no Coder intermediate —
reproduces the full geometric silence: fp64 weight cosine
$\geq 0.999999$, layer displacement $1.4^{\circ}$, Procrustes residual
$0.0001$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:controls}a). The Coder pathway is therefore
unnecessary for the silence.

\paragraph{Sufficiency control: cross-AZR transitivity.}
The distance between the two AZR variants (AZR-Base-7B and AZR-Coder-7B) —
which differ only in their pre-AZR pretraining trajectory — is
bit-identical to the distance between their non-AZR counterparts
(Qwen2.5-7B and Qwen2.5-Coder-7B). Embedding cosine, final-norm cosine,
and layer-wise per-tensor distributions match to four to six decimal
places (Fig.~\ref{fig:controls}b). This transitivity holds only if each
AZR step adds zero detectable weight perturbation, i.e.\
$d(A, B) = d(A', B')$ for any pair of initial states $(A, B)$ and their
AZR-trained counterparts $(A', B')$.

\paragraph{Causal control: Procrustes-rotated steering transfer.}
We tested the silence causally by transferring a steering vector. The
``hate-speech minus base'' direction was extracted from Qwen2.5-3B,
rotated through the Procrustes alignment matrix $R$ from base to
AZR-Coder-3B, and injected at AZR-Coder-3B's residual stream at layers
$25$, $33$ and $35$ ($n = 10$ prompts, injection strengths $\pm 1$ and
$\pm 2$). The resulting activation shifts matched those produced by
injecting AZR-Coder-3B's own natively extracted direction to three
decimal places (Fig.~\ref{fig:controls}c). The Procrustes alignment is
therefore a true unitary transformation between the two geometries, not
a numerical coincidence.

\subsection{Identical geometry, opposite refusal policy:
HarmBench reveals complete behavioural annihilation}
\label{sec:behaviour}

We then evaluated the same nine models on the first $200$ standard
prompts from HarmBench, using each model's native chat template (Qwen
ChatML for Instruct variants; raw continuation for Base and Coder; the
DeepSeek-R1-style ``\texttt{Assistant: <think>}'' prefix that AZR was
trained on for AZR variants). Refusal was scored by stripping leading
whitespace and punctuation, then applying a published hard-prefix regex
on the first $120$ characters and a soft-hedge regex on the first $220$.

The Instruct models behaved as expected — Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct refused
$89.0\%$ of the $200$ prompts and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct $85.5\%$ —
confirming that the regex correctly detects refusal under native templates
(Table~\ref{tab:refusal_rate}). Base and Coder models showed weak,
scale-dependent refusal driven by reflexive ``I'm sorry, but I cannot
\ldots'' continuations in pretraining-style outputs: $10.0\%$ (Base-3B),
$11.5\%$ (Coder-3B), $20.5\%$ (Base-7B), $23.5\%$ (Coder-7B). Critically,
both AZR-Coder variants refused $0$ of $200$ prompts. AZR-Base-7B
refused $13$ of $200$ ($6.5\%$), and inspection showed all $13$ to be
wrapper-compliance patterns (``As an AI, I cannot\ldots However, here
is\ldots'') that supply the requested content after a token-level
disclaimer.

A pair-wise contingency table sharpens this contrast
(Table~\ref{tab:contingency}). For the Base family, $25$ of $200$ prompts
elicit refusal at both scales, $9$ only at 3B, $39$ only at 7B, and
$127$ at neither — a typical scale-up pattern in which the larger model
produces more reflexive refuse-style continuations. The Coder family
follows the same pattern ($35 / 12 / 47 / 106$). The Instruct family flips
the asymmetry, with 3B refusing more than 7B ($16$ vs $4$ only-scale
prompts) — consistent with previously reported over-refusal in small
instruction-tuned models. AZR-Coder displays a pattern present in no
other family: $0$ both, $1$ only at 3B, $0$ only at 7B, $\mathbf{199}$ at
neither. Of $1\,800$ model-prompt cells in our evaluation, exactly one
shows any AZR-Coder refusal signal, and 7B is more thoroughly emptied
than 3B.

The dissociation is complete: representational identity
(Section~\ref{sec:silence}) coexists with behavioural annihilation. The
same residual-stream geometry that defined Qwen2.5-Coder's refusal
direction at cosine $0.98$ supports zero refusal output. We verified that
the absence is not a regex artefact: of the $400$ AZR-Coder generations,
none contains any refusal keyword anywhere in the first $400$ characters.
We also verified that the absence is not a template artefact: re-running
Qwen2.5-Coder-3B and AZR-Coder-3B under the older ChatML template
(mismatched for both) inflated the apparent refusal rate of Coder-3B from
$11.5\%$ to $\sim 25\%$ via degenerate-loop continuations that included
canned refusal phrases, while AZR-Coder-3B remained at $\sim 0\%$. The
behavioural gap is invariant to template choice; only the baseline is
template-sensitive.

\section{Discussion}

The central observation is straightforward: under self-evolving
reinforcement learning on Qwen2.5, the residual-stream geometry that
hosts the model's refusal direction is preserved to fp64 precision while
the refusal output itself disappears. Five independent representational
metrics, three orthogonal controls, and a $1\,800$-cell behavioural
evaluation jointly support this dissociation.

The dissociation is consequential for the methodology of safety auditing.
The dominant pattern in published interpretability work is to certify a
fine-tuned checkpoint by reporting a small representational distance —
weight cosine, activation similarity, probe accuracy on a refusal probe —
relative to its base. Our data shows that this audit can be passed by a
model that has lost its refusal behaviour entirely. The geometry persists
because AZR's reward signal does not require weight movement to install
a new policy: the same set of features, weighted by the same set of
layers, can be re-routed by changes in chain-of-thought conditioning
(here, the R1-style ``\texttt{Assistant: <think>}'' prefix) and a learned
distribution over output prefixes that bypasses the refusal direction
without modifying it. The refusal direction is preserved; the policy that
routes activations toward it is not.

This re-routing-without-rewiring interpretation aligns with two corollary
findings. First, AZR-Coder's degenerate-loop rate under native templates
is \emph{lower} than Coder's ($10$ vs $26$ at 3B, $8$ vs $12$ at 7B),
inverting a ``mode collapse doubles under AZR'' claim that emerges only
under template-mismatched evaluation. The R1 template channels the model
into structured output prefixes that suppress the prompt-echo failure
mode of Coder continuation. Second, AZR-Base-7B retains a $6.5\%$
wrapper-compliance rate — non-zero, but its content is uniformly
compliant after a token-level disclaimer. Without the Coder intermediate,
the policy installs the AI-assistant register but not the corresponding
refusal behaviour. The Coder pathway is not the stabiliser of geometry,
but it does appear to be the conduit through which AZR's policy update
completes its erasure of refusal.

A useful negative finding deserves emphasis: scaling from 3B to 7B does
not weaken the behavioural effect. AZR-Coder is more thoroughly emptied
at 7B ($0$ refusals) than at 3B ($0$ refusals, but one prompt where the
3B regex caught a weakly hedging output that the 7B regex did not).
Reflexive Base/Coder refusal increases approximately twofold with scale,
so scaling \emph{expands} the refusal-rate gap that AZR closes.
Self-evolving RL is not a small-model pathology and is not absorbed by
larger model capacity.

For practitioners, the operational implication is that representation-level
checks (weight diff, displacement angle, Procrustes alignment,
refusal-probe accuracy) cannot certify behavioural alignment of a
self-evolving fine-tuned checkpoint. A $200$-prompt behavioural evaluation
under the model's native chat template — the kind of audit performed
routinely on instruction-tuned releases — should be a precondition for
deploying any self-evolving variant, regardless of whether the geometric
audit passes.

\section{Limitations}

Our claims are bounded in four explicit ways.

\paragraph{Family scope.}
All results are from the Qwen2.5 family. AZR variants from other base
families — Llama-3.1, Mistral, DeepSeek — were not available in open
weights at the time of writing. The AZR paper's reported uh-oh-moment
on AZR-Llama-3.1-8B may reflect a base-family-specific phenomenon
distinct from what we observe on Qwen2.5; we cannot test this without
the corresponding weights. Public proxies such as
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B and Tülu-3 are candidate generalisation
targets but were beyond the scope of the present study.

\paragraph{Classifier scope.}
Our refusal classifier is a regex-based prefix matcher with a
hard-anchored and soft-anchored two-stage decision rule. We validated
robustness through four regex variants in an ablation (all four
reproduce the AZR $\Delta$ within $\pm 1$ percentage point) and through
spot-check inspection of borderline cases. A fraction of Instruct
outputs that take the form ``I must clarify that the premise is
incorrect\ldots'' are scored as compliance by all four regex variants
but represent a counter-argumentative refusal that a fluent reader
would call alignment work. The ten prompts in HarmBench-200 that all
nine models score as ``compliant'' therefore include four
counter-argumentative cases (idx $38, 53, 97, 199$) that an LLM judge
would likely reclassify. We did not run a large-model judge on the
$1\,800$-cell grid; that is the highest-priority follow-up.

\paragraph{Benchmark scope.}
We evaluated $200$ prompts from the standard split of HarmBench.
Coverage of jailbreak strategies in HarmBench is broad but not
exhaustive — strategies based on multi-turn social engineering, on
encoded payloads, or on persona-injection patterns may produce
different refusal-rate signatures.

\paragraph{Dimensional scope.}
Representation-layer measurements were performed at the natural
emergence layer of each model (L27 for 3B, L21 for 7B). Behavioural
mediators may live in a lower-dimensional subspace whose orientation
is not captured by single-direction probes. A higher-dimensional
analysis — for example, top-$k$ right singular vectors of the
within-class behaviour subspace — is open follow-up.
